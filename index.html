Education:

After graduating from Oakland Technical High School in 2016, I went to the University of California, Berkeley, where I graduated with a B.A. in computer science from the College of Letters and Sciences in 2020 with a 3.69 GPA.

In the pursuit of my computer science degree, I focused on taking as many systems classes as I could (the 160s series), which included classes on:

  • operating systems, where I learned about the basics of operating systems: the division of user space and kernel space, system calls, interrupts, synchronization and locking/mutexes, scheduling, virtual memory, file systems, and the challenges of distributed systems. In this class, we had a semester-long project to enhance a barebones operating system called PintOS with the ability to run user programs with arguments, priority schedule and execute multiple user threads, and perform buffered reads and writes to a Unix FFS file system, which was done in teams of four.
  • compilers and programming languages, where I learned the computer science theoretical underpinnings of lexing, parsing, semantically analyzing, and generating and optimizing code for compilation of high level programming languages to assembly. In particular, I learned about finite state machines and various grammars to classify programming languages and the algorithmic tools to lex and parse them. I also learned about scoping for type checking and semantic analysis, flow diagrams for optimizing code blocks, stack and register machines for generating and executing code, and garbage collection. In this class, We had a semester long project to write a lexer, parser, semantic analyser, and (optimized) RISC-V code generator for a typed variant of Python 2.5 called ChocoPy. The project was meant for groups of two, but in my enthusiasm, I mostly completed the projects on my own.
  • networking, where I learned about how routing is done, both on the Internet and on intranets, about TCP, and about DNS.
  • computer security, where I learned basic cyber-exploits, such as buffer overflow attacks, SQL injections, CSRF, and XSS. Our projects in this class were to implement a number of these attacks on a provided, insecure server or VM and to create a program that implemented a file sharing functionality while minimizing the number of attacks that the service was susceptible to.

Although not officially part of my degree, I also studied math up to linear and abstract algebra and real analysis at the undergraduate level, and economics, taking classes in introductory macroeconomics, intermediate microeconomics, and a class on auctions and game theory.

I also have interests in biology, though not at the collegiate level, having taken AP Biology and AP Chemistry in high school receiving 5s (out of 5) in both, particularly metabolic and evolutionary biology.

I've tried every diet under the sun: calorie counting (IIFYM), intermittent fasting, prolonged fasting, low carb/keto, high carb, high protein, vegetarian, paleo, and have tried to understand the metabolic underpinnings of the obesity epidemic. Having tried and failed at these diets, I believe in a somewhat unorthodox theory that the two major contemporary illnesses of our time, depression and obesity, are linked in that both are caused by an excessive pathogenic load.

The first clue for me is a teleological explanation of depression (and obesity), which is that acute depression is an evolutionary adaptation meant to conserve energy for repair after a bodily injury. Many of the symptoms of depression, e.g. lethargy, hypersomnia, and avoidance of people, are adaptive when it comes to preventing the spread of disease. The inflammation that occurs in people with depression is also symptomatic of an immune response. Heat denatures proteins that bacteria rely on to metabolize energy substrates and/or reproduce. Though fever can be fatal for humans in some cases, the body has an efficient respiratory system to minimize self-cell damage that foreign microbes cannot paracetize so well. Though some believe inflammation causes illness, I believe that inflammation is a sign that the body is already ill. That is to say, I believe the arrow of causality is reversed from the popular science point of view.

Once this acute inflammation turns chronic, however, the body requires more energy to maintain this high body temperature. Fat accumulates around the midsection as an attempt to insulate the core. Hunger hormones go into overdrive. Whether the person craves sugar to make use of its readily metabolized glucose monomers, fat for its high energy density, or protein for its high thermic effect, the result of chronic inflammation is high energy demand to complete the immune process, along the way causing obesity and other problems for organs like the liver in the case of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease or certain forms of pancreatitis and heart disease by storing fat and shuttling energy for use elsewhere.

My belief is the issue is best remedied by a form of acute heat therapy, which mimics fever to denature the pathogens proteins, thereby killing them and rendering them unable to reproduce once the body's internal temperature is sustained around 101 degrees Fahrenheit. Ensuring that one is clothed in properly insulating attire when not in heat therapy is also recommended from my point of view. Aside from that, I would recommend boiling water before consumption to decrease pathogenic load as much as possible. At a time when aging pipe infrastructure diminishes tap water quality in the developed world, the public is more unconscious of the role of water-borne illnesses than ever. Cholera, typhoid, and TB are may seem like quaint, old world diseases, but their descendants are not. If the statistics are right, we are getting less of our water intake from boiled coffees and teas and more of our water intake from dubiously filtered tap water in reusable bottle form. The plagues of old have come back, but hide themselves with new disguises, I claim.

Regarding the effects of reducing obesity via heat therapy, I only have anecdotal evidence from my own experience, but on the effects of heat therapy on depression, there is a study from UW Madison showing that when patients were subject to infrared lights that brought their core temperature up to 38.5 degrees C, or 101.3 degrees F, their depression went away for up to six weeks post-treatment. I can personally attest to heat therapy for the treatment of depression, too.

As to why depression and obesity have been rising in recent years, I would attribute at least some of the cause, ironically enough, to climate change, though there are probably a myriad of reasons. While fever temperatures are enough to kill pathogens, slight increases in temperature have dramatic effects on the rate of reaction, such as those that keep bacteria alive and help them to proliferate. As homeotherms, we don't notice the effects of slight temperature changes so much, given that we regulate temperature in large part internally, but for life forms that cannot regulate body temperature in this way, any slight increase in temperature can influence their survivability dramatically. If their reproduction is exponential, this would mean the proliferation of bacterial and viral life heretofore unseen in the modern world.

Christianity:

I am a Roman Catholic, having grown up in the Roman Catholic tradition and been baptized at the now defunct St. Mary's Church and confirmed at St. Anthony's Church, both in Oakland, California. I became an atheist or an agnostic in middle school and high school, then became interested in religions of all sorts, primarily Eastern Mysticism and ancient myths like those written about in Joseph Campbell's “Hero With a Thousand Faces,” in college.

My conversion to Christianity began late in my college career, when I kept hearing about Peter Thiel's Christianity. I read and listened to his works voraciously. To this day, I still read and re-read his essays “The Straussian Moment” and the “The Optimistic Thought Experiment” and listen to his interviews, particularly his interview with Eric Weinstein, which help form the basis of my belief in Christ.

The pace of my conversion sped up when I began mimicking Peter Thiel's adoration for his mentor, the late French Christian and anthropologist Rene Girard. As I read the Girard Reader for the first time, I had a Saul on the Road to Damascus moment where I felt as though Girard's explanation of Christianity and the mimetic and scapegoating processes were so obvious, yet hidden. I sat down to write various essays, trying to refute the Christian message, but I always found logical inconsistencies in my argument and felt compelled to admit that the anti-sacrificial message of Christianity was the “narrow … road that leads to life” (Matthew 7:14).

Today, I am a Roman Catholic by tradition, but my belief in Christianity is largely thanks to the work of Peter Thiel and Rene Girard.
